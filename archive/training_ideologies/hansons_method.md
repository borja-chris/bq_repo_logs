# Hansons Method

## Context

This brief evaluates Hansons Method for the current project context:

- Goal A race: half marathon on 2026-10-04
- Current block start: 2026-06-01
- Current working volume: about 20 miles per week
- Pre-block target: 30-35 miles per week
- Expected peak: 50-55 miles per week
- Stretch peak: 58-60 miles per week only if earned
- Known limiter: consistency and training discontinuity after gaps

Bottom line: Hansons is a strong fit as a governing framework, but only as a half-marathon-specific adaptation. The main risk is not the moderate long run. The main risk is stacking frequency, mileage, and SOS workouts faster than consistency can absorb.

## Core Philosophy

Hansons is built around cumulative fatigue. No single workout is supposed to carry the plan. Fitness comes from repeated, balanced training where the runner often carries mild fatigue but can still execute workouts.

Luke Humphrey defines the intended fatigue state as fatigue from the full training load, not from one oversized workout, while still being able to run strong. He frames the system around balance, moderate-to-high mileage, consistency, and active recovery.

Kevin Hanson has also emphasized that the method is not a "less is more" shortcut. It shifts emphasis away from a heroic long run and toward six-day frequency, total weekly volume, race-pace practice, and learning to run well on tired legs.

## Plan Structure

Classic Hansons plans commonly use an 18-week structure. The typical rhythm is:

- One rest or cross-training day
- Two quality weekday workouts
- One long run
- Several easy or active recovery runs
- A recurring weekly rhythm such as Tuesday workout, Thursday tempo or race-pace work, Sunday long run

The official Hansons Running page provides free beginner and advanced marathon and half-marathon plans. The half-marathon plans show that the method can be applied directly to the half marathon, not only borrowed from marathon training.

For the official half-marathon plans:

- Beginner Half starts at low mileage and builds into the 40s, peaking around the high 40s.
- Advanced Half reaches roughly 50 miles per week.
- Long runs are mostly 10-14 miles in the advanced half plan.
- Race-specific work is usually half-marathon pace, with later strength-style work around slightly faster than half-marathon pace or around 10K pace depending on plan level.

## Workout Types

### Easy and Active Recovery

Easy running is not filler. It maintains frequency, supports aerobic volume, and keeps the cumulative-fatigue rhythm intact. The system breaks down if easy days become moderate days.

### Speed Work

Early half-marathon work commonly uses intervals such as 400s, 600s, 800s, 1Ks, 1200s, and mile repeats. The goal is controlled speed, VO2, and running-economy development, not anaerobic racing in training. For this project, the key is to avoid speed work that compromises the rest of the week.

### Strength Work

In Hansons terminology, "strength" usually means longer running repeats slightly faster than goal race pace. It does not mean weightlifting. Marathon plans often use marathon pace minus about 10 seconds per mile; half-marathon plans use half-marathon pace minus about 10 seconds per mile or 10K pace depending on the plan.

### Tempo or Race-Pace Work

Hansons uses "tempo" differently from many training systems. In the marathon method, tempo commonly means goal marathon pace, not lactate-threshold pace. For a half-marathon adaptation, the equivalent is sustained half-marathon-pace work.

For this cycle, the race-specific emphasis should be threshold, stamina, and half-marathon pace, not a literal import of marathon-pace tempo from Advanced Hansons Marathon Method.

### Long Run

The long run is moderate, frequent, and placed inside the fatigue cycle rather than isolated as the weekly event. The famous 16-mile marathon long run is often misunderstood. The point is not that 16 miles is magic. The point is that the long run should be proportional to weekly volume and should not damage the rest of the week.

Hansons sources repeatedly frame long runs around:

- Percentage of weekly mileage
- Time on feet
- Recovery cost
- Ability to keep training before and after the long run
- Cumulative fatigue from the surrounding week

For the current half-marathon cycle, this supports mostly 13-15 mile long runs, not routine 16-18 mile long runs unless weekly volume and recovery signals clearly justify them.

## Mileage and Frequency

Hansons assumes the athlete can handle frequent running. Marathon plans commonly build toward six days per week and, in the published marathon plans, roughly 50-60+ miles per week at peak.

For this project, 50-55 miles per week fits the Hansons half-marathon universe. The stretch target of 58-60 miles per week should remain conditional because it is closer to advanced marathon-plan load than necessary for a strong half-marathon build.

## Intensity Distribution

Hansons is not purely polarized in the strict modern 80/20 sense, but most mileage should still be easy. The hard work is concentrated into SOS days:

- Speed or strength workout
- Tempo or race-pace workout
- Long run
- Easy volume around those sessions

The practical risk is that moderate fatigue turns easy days into medium-hard days. If that happens, the athlete is no longer doing the method correctly.

## Strengths

- Builds durability and rhythm.
- Rewards consistency.
- Reduces overdependence on giant long runs.
- Provides strong race-specific pacing practice.
- Teaches performance under fatigue, which matters for half-marathon late-race strength and future marathon training.
- Bridges well from half-marathon training toward a later BQ marathon arc.
- Discourages make-up workouts and single-run heroics.

## Risks

- Six-day frequency can expose inconsistency quickly.
- SOS workouts can accumulate fatigue faster than expected.
- Easy days can drift too hard.
- The plan is less forgiving after missed weeks because workouts depend on continuity.
- Adding extra long-run mileage undermines the design.
- Jumping from 20 miles per week to full Hansons load too quickly is the main failure mode.

Humphrey distinguishes functional cumulative fatigue from overreaching: being tired is acceptable if workouts remain stable; worsening performances and feeling bad are warning signs.

## Ideal Athlete Profile

Best fit:

- Already runs 5-6 days per week or can build there gradually
- Responds well to routine
- Can keep easy days easy
- Likes structured workouts
- Has enough life stability to avoid frequent missed days
- Wants race-specific pacing and late-race strength
- Is patient enough to let weekly volume work instead of forcing one big session

Poor fit:

- Inconsistent schedule
- Needs frequent cross-training instead of running frequency
- Treats every workout as a test
- Frequently stacks missed workouts
- Cannot recover from moderate weekly mileage
- Wants a long-run-centered plan

## Adaptation for the 2026 Half Marathon

Recommended adaptation:

- Keep the 18-week Hansons-inspired block.
- Build toward six days per week, but do not require it immediately if consistency is shaky.
- Use half-marathon-pace work as the main specificity.
- Use speed early, then shift toward longer strength and stamina work.
- Keep long runs mostly 13-15 miles.
- Cap peak around 50-55 miles per week unless decision gates are clearly passed.
- Treat 58-60 miles per week as optional, not aspirational.
- After missed training, restore frequency and easy volume before restoring full workouts.

For this runner, the best version is not a literal Advanced Hansons Marathon plan. It is a half-marathon-specific version with the same principles: consistency, cumulative fatigue, controlled SOS days, moderate long runs, and honest easy running.

## Fit for the Known Limiter

Hansons can be useful because it makes consistency the central adaptation. But it is also unforgiving if the runner tries to resume full load after a gap.

Fit assessment: good framework, conditional execution.

Decision rules:

- If six days per week is consistent and 45-50 miles per week feels normal, proceed toward 50-55.
- If easy days stop feeling easy, reduce intensity first.
- If SOS days degrade, reduce SOS volume before cutting all running.
- If long runs require multi-day recovery, shorten them.
- If a gap occurs, resume by restoring frequency and easy volume before restoring full workouts.
