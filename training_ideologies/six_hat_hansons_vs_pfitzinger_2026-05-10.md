# Six Hats Debate: Hansons Method vs Pfitzinger for the 2026 Half-Marathon Block

**Date**: 2026-05-10
**Rounds**: 3
**Decision**: Whether the current half-marathon block should stay Hanson-inspired, switch to Pfitzinger, or use a hybrid.

---

## Final Recommendation (Blue Hat)

Use a **Hanson-led, Pfitz-informed hybrid** for the 2026-10-04 half marathon.

The primary framework should remain Hansons because the current project already values six-day rhythm, cumulative fatigue, moderate long runs, controlled SOS work, and consistency as the top constraint. That maps cleanly to the known limiter: training discontinuity after gaps. Hansons also has a direct half-marathon method and official half-marathon plans that live near the project's expected peak range.

Borrow from Pfitzinger deliberately, not wholesale. The useful pieces are the medium-long run concept, threshold progression, disciplined aerobic volume, and occasional progression long runs. Those are valuable for the long-term BQ arc and for half-marathon performance. But a literal Pfitzinger plan, especially one modeled after Advanced Marathoning, would risk adding too much midweek endurance stress before consistency is proven.

The operational decision is:

- Keep Hansons as the organizing philosophy for the current block.
- Add one Pfitz-style medium-long aerobic run only after frequency is stable.
- Make threshold and half-marathon-pace work the main quality emphasis.
- Keep most long runs at 13-15 miles.
- Cap the cycle near 50-55 miles per week unless the established decision gates clearly support more.
- Treat 58-60 miles per week as earned optional volume, not a target to chase.

This should be revisited after 4-6 stable weeks of the block. If the athlete is consistently running six days per week, 45-50 miles per week feels normal, easy days stay easy, and SOS days are improving rather than degrading, the plan can become more Pfitz-influenced. If consistency remains fragile, stay more purely Hanson-inspired and protect rhythm over added complexity.

## Round 1

### White Hat - Facts and Constraints

The current project context is a half marathon on 2026-10-04, with an 18-week block starting 2026-06-01. Current working volume is about 20 miles per week, with a pre-block target of 30-35 miles per week, expected peak of 50-55 miles per week, and a stretch peak of 58-60 miles per week only if earned. The known limiter is consistency after training gaps, not a known injury-fragility pattern.

Hansons is already the default framework in the repo. It emphasizes cumulative fatigue, moderate long runs, six-day rhythm, SOS days, active recovery, and race-specific pacing. Official Hansons half-marathon plans exist and fit roughly within the expected peak-mileage range.

Pfitzinger is a serious aerobic-performance system. Advanced Marathoning is marathon-focused with mileage tiers from under 55 miles per week to 85+ miles per week. Faster Road Racing is the more relevant Pfitzinger source for the half marathon. Pfitzinger emphasizes aerobic workload, long runs, medium-long runs, lactate-threshold work, race-specific work, recovery, and tapering.

### Red Hat - Preference and Motivation

Hansons feels familiar and aligned with the current project identity. There is a sense of unfinished business with it: if the previous experience was real but not fully maximized, it is emotionally appealing to execute it better this time.

Pfitzinger feels tempting because it carries the aura of serious marathon preparation. It feels like the kind of system a future BQ attempt should eventually grow into. The medium-long run and threshold emphasis feel mature and substantial.

The emotional risk is chasing novelty because Pfitzinger sounds more advanced. The emotional opportunity is using Pfitzinger as a way to raise the ceiling without abandoning the rhythm that already makes sense.

### Yellow Hat - Upside

Hansons could work very well because it turns consistency itself into the training stimulus. A six-day rhythm with controlled SOS days, moderate long runs, and race-specific pacing would directly address the need to become a more continuous runner.

Pfitzinger offers a strong long-term bridge to the BQ arc. Medium-long runs, threshold development, and progression long runs all build the aerobic durability that later marathon training will demand. Borrowing these elements could make the half-marathon block more useful than a single-race build.

The best upside is a hybrid: Hanson structure keeps the week coherent, while Pfitzinger concepts enrich the aerobic development. That could produce a strong half-marathon result and also develop marathon-relevant habits.

### Black Hat - Risk Control

The biggest risk is not choosing the wrong famous plan. The biggest risk is pretending that a runner currently around 20 miles per week can absorb a mature 50-55 mile structure plus aggressive workouts just because the calendar says the block starts soon.

Hansons can become too much if the runner adds SOS density before frequency is stable. Pfitzinger can become too much if medium-long runs, general aerobic runs, and threshold workouts all drift into medium-hard territory.

The danger of a hybrid is plan creep. Taking "the best of both" can quietly become taking the stress of both. The plan needs explicit limits: one main workout, one long run, one gradually built medium-long aerobic run only if stable, and no chasing 58-60 miles per week without decision-gate evidence.

### Green Hat - Adaptation and Alternatives

The useful creative move is to treat Hansons as the operating system and Pfitzinger as a plug-in library. Hansons defines the weekly rhythm and fatigue management. Pfitzinger contributes a controlled medium-long run and a more explicit threshold progression.

Another option is a phased block. Early weeks are Hanson-dominant: frequency, easy volume, light speed, and controlled HMP work. Middle weeks introduce Pfitz-style medium-long endurance. Late weeks use Hanson-style race-specific fatigue plus selective Pfitz-style progression or threshold work.

A third option is to define two fallback versions in advance. If consistency is strong by mid-block, use the Pfitz-informed version. If consistency is uneven, revert to a simpler Hanson version with lower mileage, shorter long runs, and fewer stacked stresses.

## Round 2

### White Hat - Facts and Constraints

The research briefs agree that both systems require consistency. Neither system is a shortcut. Hansons demands frequent running and controlled SOS execution. Pfitzinger demands aerobic volume, disciplined pacing, and the ability to recover from medium-long and long endurance stimuli.

The half-marathon race distance matters. Hansons marathon tempo should not be copied literally; the current block needs half-marathon pace, threshold, and stamina work. Pfitzinger marathon plans should not be copied literally either; Faster Road Racing is the more relevant source.

The decision gates in the repo are especially relevant for any move toward 58-60 miles per week: six days per week must be consistent, 45-50 miles per week must feel normal, easy days must stay easy, SOS days must not degrade, long runs must not require multi-day recovery, and warning signs must be absent.

### Red Hat - Preference and Motivation

The hybrid feels right only if it stays simple. There is an intuitive discomfort with an overbuilt hybrid that makes every week feel like a test. The athlete probably needs confidence from repetition more than stimulation from variety.

Hansons still feels emotionally safer because it is familiar and because the current plan is already built around it. Pfitzinger feels like the future self: disciplined, aerobic, serious, BQ-oriented.

The best emotional framing is not "switching to Pfitz." It is "training like a Hanson runner who is learning Pfitzinger's aerobic discipline."

### Yellow Hat - Upside

The strongest case for Hanson-led planning is that it makes the runner practice showing up. Six days per week, easy days, and controlled SOS workouts create a behavioral foundation.

The strongest case for Pfitzinger influence is that the medium-long run could become a powerful durability builder. It also teaches that aerobic development is not only about Sunday; it is built through the week.

This combination could produce a durable half-marathoner now and a better marathon trainee later. The half marathon becomes a foundation-building step rather than a siloed race.

### Black Hat - Risk Control

The hybrid should not include three demanding stimuli plus peak mileage. If the week has a threshold workout, a medium-long run, and a long run, the medium-long run must be aerobic and controlled. If the medium-long run starts behaving like an SOS day, the week becomes too expensive.

The athlete's history of gaps means the plan must have missed-training rules. No make-up workouts. No adding missed mileage to the long run. No jumping back to full volume after interruption.

The 58-60 mile question should be kept out of the emotional center of the plan. A successful 48-55 mile cycle with continuity is more valuable than a 60-mile peak that destabilizes the block.

### Green Hat - Adaptation and Alternatives

A practical weekly architecture could be:

- Monday: off or very easy
- Tuesday: SOS workout, usually threshold, HMP, speed, or strength depending on phase
- Wednesday: easy
- Thursday: medium-long aerobic run, introduced gradually
- Friday: easy or recovery
- Saturday: easy with strides or short aerobic support
- Sunday: long run

Another architecture keeps the classic Hanson Tuesday/Thursday/Sunday SOS rhythm but lets Thursday sometimes be a Pfitz-style medium-long progression rather than a separate hard workout.

The plan can use a switchboard rule: if fatigue is normal, keep the medium-long run; if fatigue is accumulating, shorten the medium-long run; if SOS quality degrades, remove the medium-long run before removing the whole plan.

## Round 3

### White Hat - Facts and Constraints

Both systems are compatible with a 50-55 mile peak if built gradually. Neither requires 58-60 miles for the current half-marathon cycle. The current runner is not yet established at the pre-block target, so the ramp into June matters more than the label attached to the plan.

The evidence supports Hansons as the current organizing framework because it already has half-marathon plans and aligns with the existing repo assumptions. The evidence supports Pfitzinger as a valuable long-term influence because of its medium-long run, threshold, aerobic volume, and marathon continuity.

The decision should be made at the level of weekly stress architecture, not brand identity.

### Red Hat - Preference and Motivation

The emotionally satisfying choice is not abandoning Hansons. It is upgrading the existing plan with one or two mature Pfitzinger ideas. That preserves continuity while giving the runner the feeling of learning from another respected system.

There is also a gut-level warning: do not let "serious" become synonymous with "more." Serious training here means consistent, repeatable, and honestly paced.

### Yellow Hat - Upside

The recommended hybrid has a strong ceiling. It can build half-marathon-specific fitness now and lay down marathon-relevant structure for later. It also creates a learning cycle: the runner can discover whether they thrive on medium-long runs before the future marathon build depends on them.

If executed well, this block can answer several useful questions: Can six days become normal? Can 45-50 miles feel routine? Does a midweek medium-long run improve durability or erode workout quality? Are threshold sessions sustainable under cumulative load?

### Black Hat - Risk Control

The plan should fail small. If a week goes poorly, the first adjustment is to reduce the medium-long run or SOS volume, not to panic or compensate. If a gap occurs, the return protocol should prioritize easy frequency before workout restoration.

The final guardrail is to keep the long run moderate. A Pfitzinger influence should not become a license for half-marathon long runs of 16-18 miles while the weekly volume is still only moderate.

### Green Hat - Adaptation and Alternatives

Use explicit labels inside the weekly plan:

- Hanson anchor: frequency, cumulative fatigue, moderate long run, race-specific SOS
- Pfitz insert: medium-long aerobic run or threshold progression
- Recovery governor: easy-day protection and decision gates

This makes the hybrid auditable. Each workout has a reason, and if the plan gets too heavy, the Pfitz insert is the first piece to simplify.

---

## Raw Debate Data

The hats converged on a Hanson-led, Pfitz-informed approach. White Hat emphasized that both systems require consistency and that neither should be copied literally from marathon plans for the current half-marathon race. Red Hat favored continuity with Hansons while acknowledging the motivational pull of Pfitzinger as a serious long-term BQ-oriented method. Yellow Hat saw the strongest upside in using Hansons to build rhythm and Pfitzinger to deepen aerobic durability. Black Hat repeatedly warned against plan creep, medium-hard aerobic drift, premature 58-60 mile targets, and make-up workouts after gaps. Green Hat proposed a practical hybrid: Hanson as the operating system, Pfitzinger as a controlled insert through medium-long runs and threshold progression.

The final synthesis is to keep the current Hanson-inspired block, add Pfitzinger elements only where they solve a real problem, and let decision gates determine whether the plan becomes more ambitious.
