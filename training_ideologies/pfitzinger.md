# Pfitzinger / Pfitzinger-Douglas

## Context

This brief evaluates Pete Pfitzinger's training philosophy for the current project context:

- Goal A race: half marathon on 2026-10-04
- Current block start: 2026-06-01
- Current working volume: about 20 miles per week
- Pre-block target: 30-35 miles per week
- Expected peak: 50-55 miles per week
- Stretch peak: 58-60 miles per week only if earned
- Long-term goal: Boston Marathon qualifying attempt over roughly 3 years
- Known limiter: consistency and training discontinuity after gaps

Bottom line: Pfitzinger is an excellent long-term BQ-oriented philosophy, but risky as a literal near-term template if the runner is not already stable at moderate-to-high mileage.

## Core Philosophy

Pfitzinger's system is serious, aerobic, structured, and workload-driven. It is not a low-volume shortcut. The central idea is that distance racing improves through large but controlled aerobic workload, specific endurance, threshold development, and enough faster work to maintain aerobic ceiling and mechanics.

For marathon training, Advanced Marathoning is explicitly aimed at serious marathoners. It offers 12- and 18-week schedules with mileage tiers from under 55 miles per week through more than 85 miles per week. It emphasizes recovery, avoiding overtraining, marathon-pace work, long runs, medium-long runs, tapering, and race-day preparation.

For shorter road races through the half marathon, the more directly relevant book is Faster Road Racing: 5K to Half Marathon. It covers 5K, 8K-10K, 15K-10 mile, and half-marathon schedules, plus base training, recovery, tapering, masters considerations, nutrition, and cross-training.

## Plan Structure

Typical Pfitzinger structure:

- A defined buildup, often 12 or 18 weeks
- Mileage-tiered schedules rather than one generic plan
- Frequent running, commonly 5-7 days per week depending on tier
- One long run most weeks
- One midweek medium-long run in marathon plans
- Substantial general aerobic volume
- One primary quality session most weeks: lactate threshold, VO2max, race pace, or tune-up race
- Recovery runs and general aerobic runs filling the rest
- A late-cycle taper

The structure is less about cumulative fatigue as a named principle and more about systematically layering aerobic endurance, specific endurance, threshold, and sharpening while keeping recovery controlled.

## Workout Types

### Recovery Runs

Very easy running used to absorb workload. These are essential because the plan contains substantial total stress.

### General Aerobic Runs

Normal aerobic mileage. These should not be treated as workouts, but they often sit above pure recovery pace. This is one reason the system can become too demanding if the runner is undisciplined.

### Medium-Long Runs

The medium-long run is one of Pfitzinger's signature features. It provides a second weekly endurance stimulus without the recovery cost of another full long run.

In marathon plans, medium-long runs are often around 10-15 miles. For this half-marathon cycle, the useful adaptation would be more conservative: start around 7-9 miles and build toward 10-12 miles if recovery stays stable.

### Long Runs

Pfitzinger long runs are not just survival runs. They are purposeful endurance sessions run fast enough to preserve normal mechanics but slow enough to recover from.

Runner's World summarizes Pfitzinger's long-run guidance as starting around 20 percent slower than marathon pace and progressing toward about 10 percent slower in the second half. It also notes his emphasis on two substantial endurance runs in marathon training: a long run and a 14-15 mile medium-long run.

For half marathon training, long runs should usually be substantial but not marathon-style by default: often 12-16 miles depending on weekly volume, durability, and the role of the medium-long run.

### Lactate Threshold Runs

Pfitzinger gives high importance to lactate threshold for distance racing. Tempo runs around threshold are central because threshold pace is a strong predictor of performance from 10K through the half marathon and remains highly relevant to the marathon.

For this project, threshold and half-marathon-specific work would be central:

- Early block: controlled LT intervals such as 3-5 x 1 mile or 2-3 x 10 minutes
- Middle block: longer continuous or broken tempo, roughly 25-45 minutes total
- Later block: half-marathon-pace segments inside longer runs, but not every week

### VO2max Intervals

VO2max work is usually 3K-5K pace work. For marathoners, it often appears later in the cycle after aerobic and threshold foundations are established. For the current half-marathon project, it should be used sparingly to maintain speed and aerobic ceiling, not as the dominant stimulus.

### Race-Pace Work and Tune-Up Races

Marathon plans use marathon-pace segments. A half-marathon adaptation should use half-marathon pace and LT-adjacent work. Tune-up races can be useful as fitness checks but should not disrupt the consistency target.

## Mileage and Frequency

Pfitzinger is not built around minimal running. The books use current mileage as the main entry filter.

Advanced Marathoning includes tiers:

- Up to 55 miles per week
- 55-70 miles per week
- 70-85 miles per week
- More than 85 miles per week

Faster Road Racing uses the same basic idea for shorter races: schedule selection depends on existing base and capacity.

For this runner, jumping directly from about 20 miles per week into a Pfitz-style 50-55 mile peak would be the main risk. The philosophy fits the long-term BQ project, but only if the ramp is earned.

## Intensity Distribution

Pfitzinger is mostly aerobic but not purely polarized. Compared with strict easy/hard systems, Pfitz includes a lot of steady aerobic mileage:

- General aerobic runs
- Medium-long runs
- Progression long runs
- Marathon-pace or race-pace work

This is effective for strong endurance athletes, but it can punish runners who turn every aerobic run into a moderate grind.

Operational rule: recovery days must stay genuinely easy, and general aerobic days must not become quiet races.

## Strengths

- Excellent for long-term aerobic development.
- Strong continuity from half-marathon training to marathon training.
- Builds durability through frequent running and medium-long volume.
- Good fit for a BQ arc because BQ-level marathon performance is an aerobic durability problem.
- Clear structure and clear workout purposes.
- Strong lactate-threshold emphasis.
- Built-in tapering and recovery logic.
- Teaches disciplined pacing across multiple intensity zones.

## Risks

- Too much too soon if entering from low or inconsistent mileage.
- Medium-long runs can become hidden hard days.
- General aerobic pace can drift too fast.
- Distance-based workouts can become too long if not converted to time for slower or less durable runners.
- The method rewards consistency and is less forgiving when training is interrupted.
- High workload plus life stress can create nonfunctional overreaching.
- A literal marathon-plan import would overemphasize marathon-specific work for the current half-marathon race.

## Ideal Athlete Profile

Best fit:

- Already consistent at moderate-to-high mileage
- Can run 5-6+ days per week without drama
- Has enough discipline to run easy days easy
- Responds well to structure and planned workouts
- Wants to race seriously, not just finish
- Has a long-term marathon or half-marathon performance goal
- Can handle midweek medium-long runs logistically

Less ideal fit:

- Repeated training gaps
- Poor recovery habits
- Life schedule that makes medium-long weekday runs unrealistic
- Tendency to chase pace on aerobic days
- Current mileage far below the target schedule

## Adaptation for the 2026 Half Marathon

Recommended adaptation:

- Do not copy an Advanced Marathoning plan.
- Borrow Pfitz elements selectively: medium-long run, LT progression, aerobic volume, progression long runs.
- Cap long runs mostly around 12-15 miles unless durability is excellent.
- Make LT and half-marathon-specific work the main quality emphasis.
- Use VO2max sparingly, especially late in the block.
- Convert long LT sessions to time when appropriate.
- Keep peak around 48-55 miles per week unless 45-50 feels normal and recovery is stable.
- Treat 58-60 miles per week as optional and earned, not required.

The best near-term use is a Pfitz-informed half-marathon block: six-day rhythm if stable, controlled mileage progression, one threshold-focused workout, one long run, one gradually built medium-long run, and strict protection of recovery days.

## Fit for the Known Limiter

Fit assessment: excellent long-term philosophy, risky as a literal near-term plan.

Pfitzinger aligns well with the BQ arc because the marathon goal will eventually require aerobic durability, medium-long endurance, threshold strength, and disciplined pacing. The issue is timing. The current limiter is consistency after gaps, which means Pfitz should be something to grow into rather than impose immediately.

Decision rules:

- Introduce the medium-long run gradually.
- Do not add medium-long run, peak mileage, and hard LT progression all at once.
- If easy and recovery days drift faster, reduce the aerobic run intensity.
- If the medium-long run compromises the main workout or long run, shorten it first.
- If a training gap occurs, restore frequency and easy mileage before restoring full quality.
